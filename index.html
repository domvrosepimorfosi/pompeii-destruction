The destruction of Pompeii

In the year 79 AD, Pompeii was a thriving Roman resort city, home to 20,000 people living in the shadow of Mount Vesuvius.

On August 24th, the mountain exploded in one of history's most catastrophic volcanic eruptions, sending a towering column of ash and pumice 20 miles high.

For twelve hours, pumice stones and ash rained down on the city, accumulating at nearly 6 inches per hour.

Then came the deadly pyroclastic surge, a 400¬∞F wave of superheated gas and ash that instantly killed everything in its path.

When it was over, Pompeii lay buried under 20 feet of volcanic material, completely forgotten for nearly 1,700 years.

The same ash that destroyed the city perfectly preserved it, creating molds of people and animals in their final moments.

These haunting casts capture the exact positions of people as they died, some clutching loved ones or shielding their faces.

Today, Pompeii gives us an unprecedented look at Roman life, from bakeries with bread still in the oven, to vibrant wall paintings.

The city remains both a tragic tomb and an incredible time capsule, teaching us about ancient life while reminding us of nature's power.
